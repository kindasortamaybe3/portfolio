Game Of...

Interactive recreational cloud storage and organization application that facilitates the user to store, create and collaborate with others.

Project Summary

The Need

In the extreme sports world, there is a need to connect, collaborate and share experiences. These sports are more than just about taking risks, they are heavily impactful on communities and encourage many walks of life to take part. With the technology industries having such sway, it’s been proven to be difficult to get people active as everyone is busy playing games. Whether it’s on a console, their phone or their desktop, people are gaming more than ever.

The Possibilities

This could be live in the moment, or throughout the week where they can upload when they can. The game of H.O.R.S.E. had been adapted from basketball into extreme sports. If you were skateboarding, it was a game of S.K.A.T.E. and in BMX, a game of B.I.K.E. This enourages riders to progress together, create connections, grow friendships and have fun.

Results

Game Of.. helps bring sports to the user. Developing an app where they can play games interactively with their friends is the obvious solution. Although technology has taken over, Game Of… uses this as an opportunity to create a game digitally, while still requiring the user to commit to physical activity.

    The Process

    Discovery Phase

    User Surveys

    These were used to figure out who the target audience would be. Users were gathered using personal contacts, social media contacts and other means such as slack & discord channels. Importance was placed on finding users that participate in the use of cloud storage software, as described in the brief. Key information needed: What features did the user prioritise? What were they using this cloud storage for? Did they do this professionally, recreationally or both? Did they have more than one cloud storage service, if so, why? What frustrations are held with their current service How could the cloud storage services be improved Findings: 37.3% use cloud storage for personal use : This shows a potential market 73.7% use these for collaboration : Good indication for the brief Majority found the cost as the biggest turn-off to using cloud storage software Available storage and Accessibility to the storage is of high importance Other applications such as iMessage, Facebook Messenger and Whatsapp were explored as alternative sources people use for collaboration (but in a more casual sense) and found that 100% of respondents used these for file sharing in some manner. These apps would be worth exploring further. Analysis of the data showed that a critical number of cloud storage users were not pleased with their cloud services due to lack of storage for space to create and collaborate freely. Paying for a service just to store data was no longer an attractive alternative as they simply stop using them.

    Competitive Analysis

    I delved into the exploration of 3 major cloud storage companies: Dropbox Google Drive Box With this I looked at the most popular or well known cloud services. What was most interesting is that there is a gap for developing something which appeals to active hobbyists, or recreational users. Most platforms focus on instruction, learning, keeping data and rare to find one that is a platform for sharing recreationally.

    User Personas:

    To expand our understanding of user needs, exploring who our main audience were and how we can try to solve their current frustrations, was done using user personas. We could endeavor to see how Game Of… could motivate users to collaborate and interact with our services. Users had combined frustrations across the board although in slightly different forms. Knowing their goals was important but addressing pain points was paramount.

    User Stories & User Flows:

    Narrowing down the hierarchy of tasks was essential for establishing what was going to be mandatory for users to store, create, share and collaborate with the developing idea, Game Of… User Stories are an integral in developing User Flows to visually show the hierarchy of needs established in the spreadsheet and figuring out how to get from each area at greater ease and less thought processing. Although these user flows look extremely complicated, the more we integrate the tasks into an effective flow, the smoother the experience will be for the user. Sketched out on white boards before being made digital.

    Sketching & Wireframes

    Sketching up different examples to see the format of how they offered their services, was key in getting the ball rolling to formulate a direction for wireframing Game Of… After sketching some wireframes on whiteboard, these were mocked into a digital prototype to begin the first round of usability testing. 4 -UP sketching was used before exploring more integrated versions. This allowed freedom to sketch without too much analysis and offered a more freehand flowing approach.

    Low Fidelity Mockups & Prototyping

    From the wireframes I built together a series of screens as digital low-fidelity mock-ups and instigated the use of prototyping to expand on the design. It was important to insure the wireframes could function as initially intended and were user friendly enough to prevent user abandonment of the service. Usability Testing with Low Fidelity Mock-Ups: The aim of the deployed prototype was to test if the design was clear for users to navigate with ease. Key takeaways: The above screens were in need of clarity, such as a button to indicate when it was someone’s turn as the box wasn’t an obvious indicator, as well as the “pending user screen being somewhat evasive in obvious signals. It can be problematic to communicate things which may depend on imagery and colour in design, but the clearer it is here, the less it will need to depend on such assets later in the design. Connecting screens and prototyping had issues in certain areas but mainly from the user wanting to click more options which was a good indicator of interest. It can sometimes be difficult to recall that the main task is to test the main objects as described in the User Stories phase. The most useful feedback was more buttons and the users explaining in detail their understanding of how things work, to ensure I make it clearer for the next test.

    Visual Aesthetics

    Branding & Identity

    To generate the branding and identify of Game Of… the main aim was to ensure the name had clarity that this was not just a collaborative, sharing, cloud based platform; but a game. After word associations, mind maps and moodboards, the best sounding was the simplest “Game Of”.

    The dots after were representative of the idea that the brand could develop into all sorts of games and ideas depending on what the user wants. For sake of design iterations, Game Of Bike was chosen. “Game Of…” allows space for continuation of the wording while still keeping it’s own identity. The ability to abbreviate into “G.O.” sealed the deal for the decision.

    Sketching ideas that would translate well to the audience can be a love/hate process. I sketched more and felt a pull towards an iconographic image. Final logo iterations: Style Guide: Building out the style guide, brought insights as an overall look of Game Of... It offered up a wealth of knowledge about the aesthetics, and served as a constant reminder for the brands parameters. Game Of… is representative of its and the subsequent iconographic logo is representative of the general “look and feel” we were after. Circular shapes imply movement and the icon is an abstract representation of the brand. Flowing, clear and moving forward. Keeping the logo monochromatic allows for future use of colour representative of what activity you will be playing a “Game Of…”. The ability to be translated into various colours and used as an inset or watermark, without the complications of being confused with any other branding. Keeping things basic retains clarity but by including a warm shade based on orange as the main colour, helped to represent enthusiasm,determination, encouragement and stimulation. The choice of this colour came quite quickly, and shade selection took a few iterations to induce the “pop” and attention grabbing look required. The typography for titles was complimentary as the letter “g” was an important factor. For titles, Montserrat was dedicated as the font in general matched the “Game of… logo text”. For the written text it was important to keep with the “loop tail” g to have a general continuation of the icon as well. This helps ties in the type with the logo as well as the icon. It is important to disclose the use of the logo and what ways it can and cannot be done, to ensure the branding remains true to its intended use. Icons were selected in a rounded style to keep consistency with the branding.     Conclusion

    Key Takeaways:

    Game Of… is a project I feel connected to. The processes through this reassured me that user research and testing is of upmost importance. As a means of understanding the user, these tools cannot be underestimated. Creating the experiences and designing the structure that cater to the needs of the user is developed by these means. This is much more of a reliable and customised source of information for the brand, and is much more reliable. This method of specialised research ensured the development and creation of a product that was user based, user driven and as such, designed for the user in mind. Moving forward, the goal maintained it’s momentum though interacting with the users and I hope to continue developing this product and idea as a means to expand further on this application design as I believe there really is a valuable niche project market to be tapped into here.
